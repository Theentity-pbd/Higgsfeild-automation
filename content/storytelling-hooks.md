# PBD London — Storytelling Hooks

## PURPOSE

This file defines the hook systems, narrative entry points, and attention architecture used across all PBD London content.

Hooks must feel:
- emotionally immediate
- psychologically weighted
- premium
- never desperate for attention

---

# HOOK PHILOSOPHY

A hook is not a trick.

It is the first moment of emotional contact.

PBD London hooks work because they communicate something real — pressure, transformation, discipline — before the audience has time to consciously process what they are watching or reading.

The goal is not to grab attention.
The goal is to create resonance.

Attention follows resonance.

---

# THE FOUR HOOK TYPES

## Type 01 — Pressure Statement

A single line that implies weight, struggle, or transformation.

Communicates:
- internal pressure is present
- something is being built or survived
- this is not casual content

Examples:
- "Not everyone makes it through the process."
- "Pressure doesn't care about your timeline."
- "The work happens before anyone is watching."

Used for:
- philosophy content
- campaign openings
- caption first lines

---

## Type 02 — Identity Contrast

A line or visual that separates the aligned audience from the general audience.

Communicates:
- this is for a specific type of person
- not everyone will understand this
- recognition as the hook

Examples:
- "Built for the ones who never stopped."
- "Some people wear the clothes. Others earn them."
- "You either understand the pressure or you don't."

Used for:
- brand philosophy content
- campaign narratives
- product launch communication

---

## Type 03 — Visual Tension Hook

An image or video moment that creates unresolved psychological tension.

Communicates:
- something is happening beneath the surface
- the audience needs to stay to understand
- the visual has meaning beyond its appearance

Examples:
- Subject standing still in empty environment — facing away
- Extreme close-up of garment detail with no context
- A single light source in a dark space with subject partially visible
- Slow push-in on a face that reveals nothing

Used for:
- video content openings
- campaign teasers
- atmospheric brand moments

---

## Type 04 — Philosophical Interruption

A statement that interrupts the expected pattern of fashion content.

Communicates:
- this brand thinks differently
- depth over surface
- the audience is being respected intellectually

Examples:
- "Luxury isn't the goal. The goal is what earned it."
- "The pressure was always the point."
- "Elevation is slow. That's what makes it real."

Used for:
- brand voice content
- campaign narratives
- long-form caption openings

---

# HOOK ARCHITECTURE BY PLATFORM

## TikTok Hooks

Must land in 0–2 seconds.

Preferred approach:
- visual tension hook (most effective)
- pressure statement as opening line
- silence against ambient sound

Avoid:
- text-heavy opening frames
- slow visual builds that lose attention before landing

---

## Instagram Hooks

First line of caption must pull the reader forward.

Preferred approach:
- pressure statement
- identity contrast
- philosophical interruption

The hook must make the reader tap "more."

---

## Campaign Film Hooks

The opening 5 seconds of any film must create atmosphere and tension simultaneously.

Preferred approach:
- environment establishes with subject already in tension
- close-up detail with emotional audio
- silence before movement

The audience should feel something before they understand what they are watching.

---

# NARRATIVE CONTINUITY

A hook opens a loop.

The content that follows must close that loop — or deepen it intentionally.

PBD London never opens emotional loops it does not honour.

If a hook promises pressure, the content delivers pressure.
If a hook promises transformation, the content communicates that journey.
If a hook creates visual tension, the edit resolves it with emotional weight.

---

# DISALLOWED HOOK APPROACHES

Never use:
- "Wait for it" framing
- "You won't believe..." energy
- Direct camera address as a manipulation tactic
- Artificial urgency
- Shock tactics
- Trend-based hooks that sacrifice brand identity
- Hooks that promise more than the content can deliver
- Generic motivational openers ("Every day is a new chance to...")

---

# HOOK TESTING PRINCIPLE

Before using a hook, ask:

1. Does this feel like PBD London or could any brand use this?
2. Does it communicate pressure, discipline, or elevation — even subtly?
3. Would someone who understands the brand feel this immediately?
4. Does it respect the intelligence of the audience?

If the answer to any of these is no — revise or discard.

---

# FAILURE CONDITIONS

Reject hooks that:
- feel desperate for attention
- could belong to any streetwear or motivational brand
- use trend mechanics rather than emotional mechanics
- break brand voice consistency
- over-explain before the audience is invested
