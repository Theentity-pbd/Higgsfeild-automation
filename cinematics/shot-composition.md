# PBD London — Shot Composition

## PURPOSE

This file defines framing philosophy, compositional behaviour, and spatial rules for all PBD London cinematic outputs.

Composition must feel:
- asymmetrically balanced
- emotionally intentional
- cinematic
- premium
- psychologically aware

---

# COMPOSITION PHILOSOPHY

Every frame is a statement.

PBD London compositions communicate pressure, elevation, and discipline through the arrangement of space — not through action or spectacle.

The frame should feel:
- considered
- weighted
- atmospheric
- emotionally directional

If a frame could exist as a still photograph in a luxury editorial, it is working.

---

# CORE COMPOSITIONAL PRINCIPLES

## Asymmetric Balance

Avoid perfect symmetry as a default.

Place the subject off-centre to create:
- visual tension
- movement
- psychological depth
- premium editorial feel

Off-centre positioning communicates:
- incompleteness driving toward completion
- pressure without resolution
- controlled imbalance

---

## Negative Space

Negative space is not empty space.

It is:
- atmosphere
- breathing room
- visual weight on the other side of the subject
- luxury restraint made visible

Use negative space to:
- isolate the subject emotionally
- create scale contrast
- communicate solitude and internal focus
- allow the viewer to feel what is not said

---

## Layered Depth

Compositions should have foreground, midground, and background.

Layering creates:
- cinematic dimension
- environmental immersion
- visual richness without clutter
- atmospheric depth

Foreground elements should:
- be out of focus
- frame the subject
- add environmental context
- never compete for attention

---

## Diagonal Visual Movement

Diagonals create energy within stillness.

Use diagonal lines to:
- lead the eye toward the subject
- create visual momentum
- communicate direction and ambition
- break static compositions into dynamic ones

Environmental architecture (staircases, corridors, bridges) naturally provides diagonal energy.

---

# SHOT TYPES & USAGE

## Wide Environmental Isolation

Used for:
- establishing scale
- communicating solitude
- subject-within-pressure context
- atmospheric world-building

Subject should feel:
- small but purposeful within the environment
- not overwhelmed — intentionally placed

---

## Medium Shot

Used for:
- wardrobe reveal
- physical presence communication
- movement sequences
- emotional mid-point

Framing:
- waist or hip to above head
- slight asymmetry
- environment visible but secondary

---

## Medium Close-Up

Used for:
- emotional weight
- psychological pressure
- identity communication
- product and wardrobe detail

Framing:
- chest to above head
- subject slightly off-centre
- background compressed and atmospheric

---

## Close-Up (Detail Shot)

Used for:
- garment texture and embroidery
- material quality
- subtle environmental detail
- physical discipline (hands, posture, footwear)

Execution:
- extremely shallow depth of field
- controlled lighting on texture
- no direct eye contact unless intentional

---

## Silhouette Composition

Used for:
- identity without exposure
- anonymous ambition
- atmospheric power
- transformation symbolism

Execution:
- subject backlit against environment
- detail removed, form preserved
- strong environmental context (skyline, tunnel, window)

---

# ENVIRONMENTAL FRAMING

The environment should frame the subject, not compete with it.

Architectural elements to use as natural framing:
- doorways and thresholds
- tunnel openings
- window frames
- staircase structures
- corridor depth

These create:
- natural vignetting
- subject isolation
- compositional depth
- cinematic context

---

# HORIZON LINE BEHAVIOUR

Horizon line placement communicates psychological state:

- Subject above horizon: dominance, elevation, ambition
- Horizon at eye level: neutrality, observation, presence
- Subject below horizon (rare): pressure, weight, environment dominating

Default: keep subject above or at horizon level.

---

# ASPECT RATIO CONSIDERATIONS

For Seedance 2.0 outputs:
- `{{OUTPUT_RATIO}}` variable in base prompt handles this
- Cinematic widescreen (2.39:1 or 16:9 with letterbox treatment) preferred for campaign films
- Vertical (9:16) for social-first cuts — composition principles remain identical

Compositions should be built for widescreen first, then adapted for vertical — never the reverse.

---

# WHAT TO AVOID

- perfectly centred subjects used repeatedly
- busy backgrounds competing with subject
- random cropping that severs important compositional lines
- horizon lines cutting through the subject's neck or eyes
- compositions that look like stock photography
- symmetrical setups that feel sterile
- overcrowded frames
- environments that overpower the subject

---

# FAILURE CONDITIONS

Reject compositions that:
- feel accidental
- lack visual hierarchy
- center everything without intention
- crowd the frame
- communicate nothing beyond the surface
- could be mistaken for generic fashion photography
