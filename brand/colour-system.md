# PBD London — Colour System

## PURPOSE

This file defines the complete colour system for PBD London across all visual outputs — campaigns, garments, digital content, and brand communications.

The colour system must feel:
- premium
- cinematic
- controlled
- emotionally intentional

---

# COLOUR PHILOSOPHY

Colour in PBD London is not decoration.

It is atmosphere and psychological direction.

The palette was built to communicate:
- pressure
- discipline
- elevation
- restraint
- luxury grounded in reality

Every colour decision should reinforce the emotional and visual identity of the brand.

Less colour, used with more intention, is always more powerful.

---

# PRIMARY PALETTE

These colours form the foundation of all PBD London visual outputs.

---

## Black

**Role:** Dominant base. The primary environment of the brand.

Usage:
- primary background in campaigns
- base garment colour
- primary typographic colour on light surfaces
- cinematic shadow and depth

Emotion communicates:
- discipline
- power
- luxury
- pressure and weight

---

## Charcoal Grey

**Role:** Softened black. Depth with accessibility.

Usage:
- secondary background and environmental tones
- garment colourway
- supporting graphic elements
- gradient transition from black

Emotion communicates:
- maturity
- urban texture
- restraint
- controlled seriousness

---

## Silver

**Role:** Premium accent and highlight.

Usage:
- metallic details on garments (hardware, embroidery)
- typographic highlight moments
- lighting reflection in cinematic content
- logo and brand mark treatments

Emotion communicates:
- elevation
- precision
- modern luxury
- earned refinement

---

## Off-White

**Role:** Premium light. Breathing space in the system.

Usage:
- primary garment colourway (alongside black)
- typographic colour on dark backgrounds
- clean campaign space
- negative space management

Emotion communicates:
- clarity
- calm confidence
- premium simplicity
- space for thought

---

## Deep Navy

**Role:** Rich depth alternative to black. Urban and elevated.

Usage:
- secondary garment colourway
- campaign background variation
- cinematic colour grading reference
- seasonal palette variation

Emotion communicates:
- depth
- ambition
- night-time urban atmosphere
- controlled seriousness

---

## Muted Blue

**Role:** Urban atmospheric tone. Cool, grounded, modern.

Usage:
- environmental tone in campaign imagery
- secondary garment accent
- colour grading support in cinematics
- subtle atmospheric layering

Emotion communicates:
- urban clarity
- focus
- cool discipline
- modern restraint

---

# ACCENT PALETTE

Accent colours are used sparingly and always with intent.

They should:
- guide attention
- create selective emotional contrast
- support hierarchy
- never overpower the primary palette

---

## Subtle Red

**Role:** Tension, pressure, emotional urgency.

Usage:
- selective garment detail (embroidery, branding elements)
- campaign accent moments
- typographic highlight when emotional weight is required
- cinematic accent in lighting (deep, muted application only)

Tone: deep, muted, never bright or commercial.

Use sparingly. One accent moment per visual maximum.

---

## Muted Green

**Role:** Grounded contrast. Quiet distinction.

Usage:
- seasonal garment colourway
- subtle campaign accent
- environmental colour reference in cinematic content

Tone: desaturated, organic, never fresh or bright.

---

## Muted Yellow / Faded Amber

**Role:** Warmth under restraint. Earned comfort.

Usage:
- late-night city lighting reference in cinematics
- rare garment accent
- selective typographic warmth in dark environments

Tone: faded, amber-leaning, never golden or bright.

---

# COLOUR USAGE RULES

## Proportion System

- Black and charcoal: 60–70% of any composition
- Off-white and silver: 20–30%
- Navy and muted blue: supporting presence
- Accent colours: maximum 5–10% of any single composition

---

## Saturation Rules

All colours should lean toward:
- desaturated
- muted
- refined

Avoid:
- oversaturated versions of any palette colour
- bright commercial applications
- colours that feel loud or cheap

---

## Combination Rules

Approved primary combinations:
- Black + Off-White (core, most used)
- Black + Silver (premium, campaign-focused)
- Charcoal + Deep Navy (atmospheric, cinematic)
- Off-White + Muted Blue (clean, urban)

Accent integration:
- always against a primary palette background
- never combined with another accent colour
- must feel motivated by the content, not decorative

---

# COLOUR IN GARMENTS

## Core Garment Palette

Primary colourways:
- Black
- Off-White / Cream
- Charcoal

Secondary colourways (seasonal):
- Deep Navy
- Muted Blue
- Stone / Sand (off-white variation)

Accent garment details:
- Silver hardware and embroidery
- Subtle Red embroidery or labelling
- Muted Green as seasonal variation

---

## Colourway Combinations on Garment

Preferred:
- monochrome (single primary colourway with tonal variation)
- two-colour maximum per garment (primary + one accent)
- tonal embroidery (slightly lighter or darker than base)

Avoid:
- clashing accent combinations
- more than two active colours on a single garment
- bright accent colours that reduce the premium feel

---

# COLOUR IN DIGITAL

## Campaign Imagery

Colour grading should reflect the primary palette:
- deep blacks, cool midtones, controlled highlights
- muted environmental colours
- skin tones natural and premium
- accent colour preserved when environmentally present

## Social Media

Consistent grade across all posts.
No single post should introduce a colour that breaks the established visual world.

## Website

- Primary: Black and Off-White
- Secondary: Charcoal and Silver
- Text: Off-White on dark, Charcoal on light
- Accent: Subtle Red for selective interactive elements

---

# WHAT TO AVOID

- oversaturated versions of any palette colour
- random colour combinations not in the system
- loud gradients
- rainbow or multi-colour treatments
- colours that feel youthful, cheap, or trend-driven
- colour misuse that undermines luxury positioning

---

# FAILURE CONDITIONS

Reject colour decisions that:
- introduce colours outside the palette without strong justification
- use accent colours as primary visual elements
- create a visual feel inconsistent with premium, cinematic brand identity
- prioritise trend over consistency
